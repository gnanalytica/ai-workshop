Data Visualization is the art and science of turning raw data into visuals that communicate meaning instantly. Today's session covers how the eye processes visuals, how to make information engaging, which chart types to use, and where visualizations are used in the real world.

### Learning objectives

- Understand how the brain processes visual information.
- Identify which visualization type fits different data stories.
- Apply design principles to make visuals clearer and more engaging.
- Recognise real-world use cases across industries.

### What we'll cover

- What your eye sees first and why it matters.
- Ways to keep visuals interesting and information-rich.
- The main types of visualizations and when to use each.
- Where data visualization is applied across industries and tools.

**Key themes.** Visual perception · Chart selection · Storytelling with data · Design principles · Real-world application.

## 🎯 Today's objective

**Topic.** Data Visualization — perception, chart choice, design principles, and real-world application.

**By end of class you will have:**
1. A working mental model for **what your eye sees first** and why.
2. A shortlist of **chart families** and when each one fits.
3. **One visualization** of your own that passes the 5-second rule.

> *Why this matters.* A clear chart earns the few seconds an audience gives you. A muddled one wastes them. Visualization is the bridge between data and decision.

### 0–10 min · Opening activity — *What do you see first?*

Three images on screen, one at a time. Students call out the first thing they notice. Then we name the **pre-attentive attributes** — *colour, shape, size, position* — and explain how the brain processes them before conscious thought.

### 10–25 min · The power of one visual

- **Florence Nightingale — Rose Chart (1858).** Convinced the British Army that more soldiers died from preventable disease than from battle wounds.
- **John Snow — Cholera Map (1854).** Localized a London outbreak to a single contaminated water pump.
- Each is a *single well-designed visual that changed a real-world decision.*
- Introduce the **5-second rule** — your audience should understand the point within five seconds.

### 25–45 min · Ways to keep things interesting

Design techniques that pull weight without adding noise:

- **Colour to encode meaning** — not decoration.
- **Layering information progressively** — start with the headline, reveal detail on demand.
- **Small multiples** — repeated tiny charts side-by-side beat one busy chart.
- **Sparklines** — trend in the space of a word.
- **Combining chart types** thoughtfully (e.g. bar + reference line).
- Walk through a **before-and-after**: cluttered chart vs. clean one. Define **chartjunk** — decoration that doesn't carry information — and show why removing it works.

### 45–60 min · Types of visualizations

The seven families and where each fits:

| Family | Use when you want to show… | One strong example |
|---|---|---|
| **Comparison** | One category vs. another | Bar chart |
| **Trend** | Change over time | Line / area chart |
| **Part-to-whole** | Composition | Stacked bar (skip pies above 4 slices) |
| **Distribution** | Spread of one variable | Histogram, box plot |
| **Relationship** | Two variables interacting | Scatter plot |
| **Geography** | Where things happen | Choropleth / symbol map |
| **Network** | Connections between things | Node-link diagram |

Run a quick **"which chart would you use?"** quiz with 3 scenarios.

### 60–75 min · Where visualizations are used

One example from each domain:

- **Business** — sales dashboards, funnel analysis.
- **Healthcare** — patient outcomes, public-health surveillance.
- **Education** — student progress, learning analytics.
- **Journalism** — election results, climate explainers.
- **Product design** — usage heatmaps, A/B test readouts.
- **AI / ML** — model performance, embedding spaces, attention maps.

End with a quick **tool overview** — no-code (Datawrapper, Flourish), code-based (Observable Plot, Recharts, D3), design-first (Figma, Illustrator), and AI-assisted (ChatGPT data analyst, Claude with CSV uploads).

## 🧪 Lab · Build one visualization

1. **Pick a dataset** you actually have access to — phone screen time, a public CSV from Kaggle, a survey result, or a simple personal log.
2. **Pick the chart type** that fits the story you want to tell. Use the table above; don't just default to a bar chart.
3. **Build it** in a tool of your choice — Datawrapper, Flourish, Observable Plot, or even a spreadsheet.
4. **Apply at least one design principle** from class — colour, hierarchy, or simplicity. Be ready to name which one and why.
5. **Test it with the 5-second rule.** Show it to someone who wasn't in class. Ask: *"What does this chart say?"* If they can't answer in 5 seconds, redo.
