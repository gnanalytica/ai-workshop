By now your capstone has *some* data — even if it's 12 rows from your own test usage. Today you make it readable. Data visualization isn't about D3.js — it's about picking the chart that makes the answer obvious.

## 🎯 Today's objective

**Topic.** Data Visualization — chart picking, encoding, and design for three audiences.

**By end of class you will have:**
1. **Three charts** built from your capstone data: user, ops, deck.
2. A clear answer for each: *"What's the one question this chart answers?"*
3. One chart embedded live in your capstone URL.

> *Why this matters.* Demo Day judges decide in 7 seconds whether your deck is worth reading. A clear chart buys those 7 seconds. A bad chart loses them.

## ⏪ Pre-class · ~20 min

### Setup

- [ ] Free **Observable** account (for Plot) OR your Next.js capstone with **Recharts** installed.
- [ ] Free **Datawrapper** account (for the pitch chart — no code).
- [ ] One CSV from your capstone: 50–500 rows is fine. If you have nothing, export your dashboard's analytics page.

### Primer

- **Read (10 min):** Datawrapper's chart-type guide. Pay attention to "compare", "show change", "show distribution", "show parts of a whole".
- **Skim:** the Observable Plot examples gallery.

### Bring to class

- [ ] Your CSV.
- [ ] One sentence for each audience: *"User wants to know ___. Ops wants to know ___. Investor wants to know ___."*

> 🧠 **Quick glossary.** **Encoding** = mapping data fields to visual properties (x, y, color, size). **Marks** = bars, dots, lines. **Channels** = position > length > color > size in human accuracy. **Chartjunk** = decoration that doesn't carry information.

### The chart-picker shortlist

| Question | Chart |
|---|---|
| Compare across categories | **Bar** (horizontal if labels are long) |
| Change over time | **Line** (or area if cumulative) |
| Distribution of one variable | **Histogram** or **box plot** |
| Relationship between two | **Scatter** (add color for third) |
| Parts of a whole | **Stacked bar** (skip pies above 4 slices) |
| Geographic | **Map** with choropleth or symbols |

### Three audiences, three encodings

- **User chart** — in-product. Single number + a trend. They want *"is this up or down?"*
- **Ops chart** — internal dashboard. Multiple series. They want *"where's the anomaly?"*
- **Deck chart** — for the pitch. One bold encoding. They want *"the headline."*

### Five sins to never commit

1. Truncated y-axis on bars (lies about magnitude).
2. Dual y-axes (humans can't read them).
3. 3-D pie charts (every angle lies differently).
4. Rainbow color scales (perceptually nonlinear).
5. Tiny labels on a 4K dashboard (test on your phone).

## 🧪 Lab: Three charts, one CSV

1. **Pick** your CSV. Identify the 1–2 fields that matter most.
2. **User chart (10 min).** In Recharts (if web) or Observable Plot (if data-only): one number + a small trend line. Embed it in your capstone.
3. **Ops chart (10 min).** Multi-series time chart or anomaly callout. Stays in your `/admin` or notebook.
4. **Deck chart (10 min).** Datawrapper. One bold headline. Export as PNG. Replace the chart on your Day-19 deck's "user" slide.
5. **Critique** (peer pairs, 5 min each): your partner names the one question your chart answers. If they can't say it in one sentence, redo.

**Artifact.** Three chart screenshots + one paragraph each — *"What this chart makes obvious"* — in `viz.md` in your repo.

> ⚠️ **Don't show three charts to the same audience.** Each one is for a different person.
